Samurai

history

From Japanese "samurai" is translated as "a person who serves."

The origin of the samurai

The samurai emerged as a result of the Taika reforms that kicked off in the Land of the Rising Sun in 646. These reforms can be called the largest socio-political transformations in the history of ancient Japan, which were carried out under the leadership of Prince Naka-no Oe.

In the X-XII centuries, in the process of "showdowns" of the feudal lords, influential families were formed. They had their own rather significant military detachments, the members of which were only nominally in the service of the emperor. In fact, every major feudal lord then needed well-trained professional warriors. They became the samurai. During this period, the foundations of the unwritten samurai code "The Way of the Bow and the Horse" were formed, which was later transformed a clear set of rules "The Way of the Warrior" ("Bushido").

A great impetus to the strengthening of samurai was given by emperor Kammu at the beginning of the ninth century. This emperor turned to the existing regional clans for help in the war against the Ainu - another people who lived on the islands of the Japanese archipelago. By the way, there are only a few tens of thousands of Ains left.

The era of the Minamoto and Edo shogunate

The final formation of the samurai as a special privileged class took place, according to most researchers, during the reign of the House of Minamotoin the Land of the Rising Sun (this is the period from 1192 to 1333). Minamoto's accession was preceded by a civil war between the feudal clans. The very course of this war created the preconditions for the emergence of the shogunate - a form of government with a shogun (that is, a military leader) at its head.

It is believed that the "golden age" of samurai was the period from the first shogun to the Onin civil war (1467-1477). On the one hand, it was a fairly peaceful period, on the other, the number of samurai was relatively small, which allowed them to have a good income.

With the coming to power of the Tokugawa house, the samurai class increased significantly - almost every fifth Japanese became a samurai. Since internal feudal wars are a thing of the past, the military units of the samurai at this time were used mainly to suppress peasant uprisings.

The decline
of the samurai
estate

The beginning of the collapse of the samurai estate is due to the fact that daimyo no longer need large personal detachments of warriors, as it was during the period of feudal fragmentation. As a result, many samurai were left out of work, turned into ronin (samurai without a master) or ninja - secret assassins-mercenaries.

After the Meiji bourgeois revolution of 1867-1868, the samurai, like other feudal estates, were officially abolished, but for some time they retained their privileged position. Those samurai who, even under the Tokugawa, actually owned the land, after the agrarian reforms of 1872-1873, legally secured their rights to it. In addition, the ranks of officials, army and navy officers, etc., were replenished with former samurai.

And in 1876, the famous "Decree on the Prohibition of Swords" was issuedin Japan. It directly prohibited the wearing of traditional edged weapons, and this ultimately "finished off" the samurai. Over time, they have become just a part of history, and their traditions have become an element of the unique Japanese flavor.

Appearance

What medieval samurai looked like is reliably known from many sources. For many centuries, their appearance has hardly changed. Most often, samurai wore wide trousers, resembling a skirt in cut, with a bun of hair on their head, called motodori. For this hairstyle, the forehead was shaved bald, and the remaining hair was braided into a knot and secured at the crown.

With regard to weapons, over the course of a long history, samurai have used different types of weapons. Initially, the main weapon was a thin short sword called a chokuto. Then the samurai switched to curved swords, which eventually transformed into the katanas known all over the world today. In the Bushido code, it was said that the soul of a samurai is contained in his katana. And it is not surprising that this sword was considered the most important attribute of a warrior.

In addition to swords, bows were also used by samurai , since with the development of military affairs, personal courage, the ability to fight the enemy in close combat, began to matter much less. And when gunpowder appeared in the 16th century, bows gave way to firearms and cannons. For example, popular during the Edo period were the flintlock rifles called tanegashima.

An integral part of the armor was Kabuto's helmet. Its cup-shaped part was made of metal plates, connected with rivets. An interesting feature of this helmet is the presence of a comforter (just like Darth Vader from "Star Wars"). It protected the wearer's neck from possible blows from swords and arrows. Together with helmets, samurai sometimes wore gloomy Mengu masks - to intimidate the enemy.
